AC – DC Full Wave Converter

EE 101A — Circuits I · Fall Quarter, Sophomore Year

← Back to Projects

In my EE 101A Circuits I class that I took in the Fall Quarter of my sophomore year, I gained exposure to basic circuitry as well as a deep understanding of the electrical components that I would use in my circuits — such as Capacitors, Diodes, and Transistors — to help achieve our end goal of rectifying a 120 V AC input to a 20 V DC output, usable for everyday consumer products such as charging one's phone. You can think of this AC – DC full wave converter as acting similar to the charging brick that helps regulate the voltage that is appropriate for your items.

Building the Full-Wave Rectifier

In building our AC-DC converter, we started off by soldering four diodes to form a full-wave rectifier. The diodes work in pairs to ensure that our voltage output remains positive, no matter how our AC voltage behaves.

When our AC voltage is in its positive half cycle, one pair of diodes allows current to flow through to the output. When it changes to its negative half cycle, the first pair turns off and another pair turns on, switching the direction of the current so that our voltage output remains positive.

For reference, negative voltage doesn't mean that we're losing voltage — it simply represents the change in direction of current flow. Diodes are arranged in a way that turns any AC wave into a positive DC waveform.

Smoothing with a Capacitor

However, our DC voltage is still unstable — we want a steady voltage. We then include a capacitor to create the waveform as measured on the oscilloscope below.

By adding a capacitor in parallel with our voltage output, we no longer see full waves at the output. Instead, we notice a ripple effect created by the capacitor at the end of the circuit. The capacitor's electrical property of resisting sudden changes in voltage helps smooth out the waveform, giving us a more constant DC output. We then add a resistor connected to the positive node of the capacitor to help discharge any stored energy the capacitor retains over time.

Regulating the Output

To reduce the ripple waves and make our DC voltage constant, we added a Zener diode. The Zener diode steps down the voltage by clamping it to a fixed voltage specified in its datasheet, keeping the output voltage around that level.

We then add a potentiometer in parallel with the Zener diode to add some variability, allowing us to adjust the output voltage to meet our specific needs.

The last part of the lab is to add a transistor at the end of our circuit, where it serves as a voltage buffer. At the gate of the transistor, the Zener diode and potentiometer set a reference for what the output voltage should be. The transistor then adjusts the current it delivers so that the output voltage closely matches this reference — keeping the DC output nearly constant, even when the load resistance becomes heavier.

We then add two other electrical components to the source of our transistor to remove any leftover AC ripple that may have passed through. The resistor in parallel ensures that the capacitor is properly discharging its stored energy, helping maintain a steady DC output.
